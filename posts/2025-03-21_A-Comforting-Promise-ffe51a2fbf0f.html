A Comforting Promise

Will They Truly Listen?
“If I say how I feel, would they listen?”

”They say they will always be there, that they will stand by my side whenever I need them.”

It’s a comforting promise — the kind I want to believe when everything feels too heavy to carry alone. But in the quiet moments, when I am left with my thoughts, doubt begins to creep in. If I share how I feel, would they truly listen?

When Speaking Feels Like a Risk

“I don’t tell people that I’m not okay because it’s hard watching them not know what to do. Then I end up comforting them, even though the one who needed comforting was me.”

So, I hold it in. Not because I want to, but because I fear what happens if I don’t. The thought of opening up only to be met with distraction, impatience, or discomfort is far more painful than keeping everything bottled inside.

They mean well. I know they do. But there’s a difference between being around and truly being with someone. A difference between hearing and listening. Between responding and understanding.

I wonder if they realize how much it hurts to feel invisible even when I am speaking. How painful it is to hear polite responses instead of real understanding. How lonely it feels to be surrounded by people who are there in presence, but absent in spirit.

A Silent Plea for Understanding

I don’t need solutions. I don’t need someone to tell me it’s going to be okay. I need someone who is willing to sit in the silence with me, to hear the weight of my unspoken words, to understand that sometimes, the most comforting thing is simply knowing that my pain is seen.

“I don’t need someone to fix me — I just need someone who won’t turn away.”

Someone who listens not just with their ears, but with their soul. Someone who doesn’t try to fix or dismiss my feelings, but simply acknowledges them.

Because sometimes, the most comforting thing is not advice, not solutions, not words of encouragement — but simply the knowledge that I am not alone in my pain. That someone truly sees me, without rushing to make the moment easier for themselves.

“So, I stay quiet. Not because I don’t want to be heard, but because I am afraid of being unheard.”

I keep my thoughts to myself. Not because they don’t matter, but because I’d rather carry them alone than place them in careless hands.

Still, a part of me hopes. Hopes that one day, when I let the words slip past my lips, they won’t fall into the void. That someone will hear, not just with their ears, but with their heart.

“Maybe, one day, they will listen — not just to my words, but to what I am trying to say beyond them.”
