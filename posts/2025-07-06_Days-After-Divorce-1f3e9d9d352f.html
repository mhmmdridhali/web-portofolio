Days After Divorce

Doubting Decisions
“Is this right or wrong?”

That’s the question that doesn’t just knock — it barges in.
Uninvited. Repeatedly. Mercilessly.

But as time passes, slowly but surely, the question of right and wrong rarely sides with the one who actually lived through the marriage.

Doubting Decision

The mind is rarely neutral ground.
Post-divorce, it splits into factions: the rational, the emotional, the wounded, the hopeful. All arguing over the same question:

Was this the right decision?

And like most things in life, “right” and “wrong” aren’t universal — they’re filtered through religion, culture, family history, and individual pain.

Wrong — because of pity for the child who sees their parents no longer together.

That’s one of the loudest refrains. Divorce is framed as failure — not just of the marriage, but of parenthood. But here’s the thing: no one ever asks the children the right question.

Can they really live with parents who no longer love each other? Or would they rather have parents who still show up, even if they live apart?

Wrong — because God hates divorce.

A common interpretation. But in Islam, divorce is not a matter of right or wrong. It is allowed, even if disliked by God. A last resort when repeated attempts at fixing things always fail.
Even God is fair and full of compassion.
Even God always gives choices.
Even God doesn’t sentence His servants.
Even God understands not every human can endure.
Even God knows failure is part of being human.

Wrong — because you should’ve been more grateful.

Grateful for a partner who was “good enough.”
Grateful for stability, for financial security, for the absence of bruises.
But what about emotional neglect?
What about trauma bonding disguised as loyalty?

The truth? Gratitude doesn’t fix dysfunction. And survival isn’t the same as happiness.

The People Who Don’t Take Your Side

After you doubting yourself, you face the world.
And most of the world is not on your side.

Some are confused.
Some are disappointed.
Some hide judgment behind faux concern:
“Are you sure this was the only way?”

They’ll tell you they’re neutral. They’re not.
Most people lean toward preservation — even if what’s being preserved is toxic.

You’ll hear it all:

“But you looked so happy on Instagram.”

“I thought he/she was the one.”

“Couldn’t you just stay for the kids?”

The hardest part isn’t answering.
 It’s realizing that some people only valued your marriage for how it looked — not how it felt.

Even family can fail to stand beside you. Especially if the image of an “intact” household matters more than the mental health of the people inside it.

The Quiet Is Not Peaceful

Then comes the stillness.
No more shared routines. No more familiar footsteps. No more presence in the other room. It’s not freedom at first. It’s numb.

You don’t miss the person — you miss the shape their existence gave your days.

Even a toxic rhythm is still a rhythm. And breaking it feels like vertigo.

You’ll scroll through your phone, not sure who to text.
You’ll eat in silence, overthinking every bite.
You’ll fall asleep to the sound of your own thoughts — unfiltered, sharp, and unrelenting.

This isn’t romanticized solitude. This is grief.
Not just for the person, but for the version of yourself that tried so hard to make it work.

And So — Was It Right or Wrong?

That question never really goes away.
Some days, it feels like liberation.
Some days, like failure.

Right and wrong aren’t fixed positions. They’re moving targets shaped by perspective, timing, and survival. Right and wrong are subjective — depending on whose standards you’re using. Maybe divorce is seen now as an act of self-preservation. And maybe not.

So — who gets to decide?
