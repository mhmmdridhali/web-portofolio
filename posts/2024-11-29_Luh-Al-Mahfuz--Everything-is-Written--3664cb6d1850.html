Luh Al Mahfuz: Everything is Written?

Man Proposes, God Disposes
“If everything is already written in Luh Al-Mahfuz, then why should we wish?”

The question feels like stepping into a paradox: if destiny is fixed, are our choices even real? And if the outcome is already known, what’s the point of hoping, praying, or dreaming? It’s a thought that confuses the edges of our understanding about free will, fate, and the divine.

Luh Al-Mahfuz

Luh Al-Mahfuz is a term in Islam referring to the concept of a tablet or record where all events in the world have been written, from major occurrences to the smallest details. Literally, the term Luh Al-Mahfuz comes from the Arabic words Luh, which means a broad surface suitable for writing, and Mahfuz, which means preserved.

Thus, the term can be interpreted as a preserved record, where everything written within it will neither change nor be corrupted.

And with Him are the keys of the unseen; none knows them except Him. He knows what is on the land and in the sea. Not a leaf falls but that He knows it. And no grain in the darkness of the earth and no moist or dry thing but that it is [written] in a clear record.” (Q.S. Al-An’am 6:59)

Interestingly, mystics outside Islam have their interpretations of a similar concept. Western esotericism refers to it as the Akashic Records, which translates to “sky records.”

Rooted in metaphysical traditions, this idea suggests a cosmic library documenting every action, thought, and event in the universe— whether they have happened in the past, are happening in the present, or will happen in the future.

In more modern perspectives, Luh Al-Mahfuz is sometimes referred to as the “universal server,” a term used by some to explain this concept in modern language — using analogies that are easier for the general public to understand.

However, Luh Al-Mahfuz is certainly far more complex and meaningful than the term “universal server.”

The Essence of Luh Al-Mahfuz

When we say that everything is written in Luh Al-Mahfuz, it truly means everything. This includes the branches of life paths that could have happened if someone made certain choices, but didn’t happen because they chose differently.

For instance, suppose You are offered coffee and tea, each with its reward. Let’s say coffee leads to receiving shoes, and tea leads to receiving clothes. Your choice is up to you, but the outcomes for both options are already written. When you choose tea, you activate the path leading to clothes, while the path leading to shoes remains a possibility that never materializes.

This is the essence of “Man proposes, God disposes.” Your choices are part of the plan, yet their outcomes have already been determined. Whether a decision is made or not, every possibility exists in Luh Al-Mahfuz.

Every possibility already exists in Luh Al-Mahfuz, whether it will happen, has happened, or will never happen.

All activities of human life are already written by God from A to Z, along with the many branches of possibilities and choices humans can make. If you choose A, the result is destiny A. Choices B and destiny B, along with the rest, are eliminated even though they are also recorded in Luh Al-Mahfuz.

‘As You Wish’

Wishing and praying are not attempts to rewrite destiny but acts of faith and connection. They acknowledge our dependence on Allah and reflect our hopes. Within the grand tapestry of destiny, they remind us that while Allah’s knowledge is absolute, our journey is still deeply personal.

Luh Al-Mahfuz is a reminder of Allah’s omniscience and mercy. It shows us that every possibility is known to Him, and yet, He grants us the freedom to choose. This divine record is not meant to limit us but to guide us in trusting His greater plan.

As we navigate life’s choices, let us remember that our wishes, prayers, and efforts are all meaningful parts of this journey. They reflect our sincerity, humility, and reliance on Allah’s wisdom.

Then,

“If everything is already written in Luh Al-Mahfuz, then why should we wish?”

Maybe, on some pages, Allah has written, ‘As you wish’.
