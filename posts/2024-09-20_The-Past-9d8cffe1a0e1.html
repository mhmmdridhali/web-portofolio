The Fine Line Between "Was" and "Used to Be"
Have you ever thought about the difference between “was” and “used to be”?

Both terms suggest that something has passed, but there’s a subtle distinction between them. “Was” states a fact, something that simply existed. But when we say “used to be,” there’s a sense of loss, a feeling that something once held meaning but is no longer present.

Why does "used to be" feel so much more painful?

The Emotional Weight of “Used to Be”

“Was” acknowledges that something has ended. It’s a simple, factual statement, like noting the end of a day. But “used to be” goes further. It suggests that the thing in question had meaning, that it once mattered. It lingers in our hearts, highlighting a change, a sense of what has been left behind.

In fact, studies show that our brains process loss in complex ways, intertwining our emotions with memories. When something we cared about is no longer part of our present, the emotional toll can last far longer than we realize. The feeling of nostalgia is a powerful reminder of how much we’ve lost and how much we once valued.

Why Do We Hold On?

It’s human nature to hold on to the past, especially to things that brought us joy or comfort. The phrase “used to be” embodies our desire to cling to those memories, to hold on to what we loved. But in doing so, we often keep ourselves from fully embracing the present.

“The hardest part of moving forward is not looking back.”

It’s as if by remembering what "used to be," we’re trying to keep a part of it alive. Yet this emotional attachment can sometimes hinder our ability to move forward, keeping us locked in a space where the past feels more real than the present.

So how do we balance remembering with moving on?

It’s not about forgetting what "used to be," but about accepting that while the past shaped us, it no longer defines us. We can cherish those memories without letting them weigh us down.

Moving forward requires us to acknowledge the difference between “was” and “used to be.” “Was” is a closed chapter, while “used to be” is a story we may still carry in our hearts, even as we write new ones.

Embracing The Present

The real question we should ask ourselves is: what will today become? As time passes, today will either turn into a simple "was"—a day that came and went—or a "used to be," filled with the emotional weight of moments that mattered.

We can either live today with purpose, creating memories that linger in our hearts, or let it slip by as just another day. Time moves forward regardless, but how we experience it defines what we carry into tomorrow.

“Sometimes you will never know the value of a moment until it becomes a memory.” – Dr. Seuss
